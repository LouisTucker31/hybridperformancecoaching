Why Lifting and Running Don't Have to Compete

20 July 2026 · 6 min read

The two get treated as opposites more often than they should. Lift hard and your running suffers. Run hard and your squat feels flat the next day. It's a common enough experience that a lot of people just pick one, but the conflict is mostly a structure problem, not a biology problem.

Why they get treated as opposites

Most of the friction comes from three things happening at once: too much fatigue stacked in the same 48 hours, no clear priority for the week, and generic templates that were never built to hold both. Add a hard leg day on top of a hard run and neither one gets your full output. Do that for a few weeks running (excuse the pun) and it's easy to conclude the two just don't mix.

There's a real physiological effect here too, usually called the interference effect, where endurance training can blunt some of the adaptations you'd otherwise get from strength work. It's real, but it's also smaller and more manageable than people assume, and it mostly shows up when volume and intensity in both disciplines are high at the same time, with no thought given to how the week is sequenced.

The actual fix is sequencing, not choosing

You don't need to drop one to protect the other. What you need is enough separation between the sessions that actually compete for the same resources, and enough restraint on the days that don't need to be hard. A lifting-focused week can absolutely hold two structured runs without your squat numbers moving backwards, as long as the week is built with that in mind rather than bolted together after the fact.

The goal isn't fitting running around lifting, or lifting around running. It's building a week where each one has a clear job.

How I actually structure a week

This is the rough shape I use with lifters adding running for the first time, and it's the same logic behind the 5K Plan for Lifters:

  • Heavy leg day and your hardest run of the week never sit back-to-back. Put at least a day, ideally an easy or rest day, between them.
  • Easier runs pair well with upper body days, since they don't compete for the same lower-body recovery.
  • Only one truly hard running session a week to start with. Everything else is easy, conversational pace, there to build the habit and the aerobic base without adding fatigue you can't recover from.
  • Long run (when you get to that point) goes as far from squat or deadlift day as the week allows, not immediately after.
  • Protein and sleep stay non-negotiable. Adding a second training discipline without adjusting recovery is the fastest way to end up flat in both.

What it looks like when it's working

You should be able to finish a run without your legs feeling like they've got nothing left for the gym in a day or two, and finish a heavy squat session without your next run feeling like wading through sand. Some crossover fatigue is normal, that's just training, but it shouldn't be compounding week on week. If it is, that's a sign the sequencing needs adjusting, not that hybrid training doesn't work for you.

The other marker worth watching is simply whether you're recovering between sessions, not just surviving them. If you're constantly starting a session already tired from the last one, the fix is almost always spacing and volume, rarely the combination itself.

Takeaways

  • The interference effect is real, but it's a dosing problem, not a reason to pick one discipline over the other.
  • Keep your hardest lifting and hardest running sessions apart in the week.
  • Start with one hard run a week, everything else easy, while you build the aerobic base.
  • Recovery (sleep, protein, and genuinely easy days) is what makes the combination sustainable.
